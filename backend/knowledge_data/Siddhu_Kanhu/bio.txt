Siddhu–Kanhu
Great heroes who learned the saga of bravery through the blood of the British
As the pressure of the noose around their neck increased, their resolve became even stronger. There was neither fear of death on their faces nor any sign of hesitation. Their eyes shone with determination, and their chests were filled with pride. They were happy that they were attaining a heroic death and becoming immortal.
This is the story of two brothers, Siddhu and Kanhu, the heroes of the Santhal Hul Revolution of 1855 A.D., who took up arms against the British-backed exploitative moneylender system and gave a strong reply. At the same time, they embraced death for their soil, for their motherland.
It must have been around the year 1820 A.D. when, under the orders of the then Collector of Bhagalpur district in Bihar, Augustus Cleveland, a Company officer named Francis Buchanan crossed the hills of Rajmahal and arrived in the plains.
This was a region where many tribal villages existed. Bhognadih was one of these villages, where Siddhu–Kanhu were born into a tribal family. During the period in which these two were born, the tribal people there earned their livelihood by bringing firewood from the forest and selling it in the market, and through farming-related work. For entertainment, they liked hunting.
Cause of the Movement: The Arrival of Francis Buchanan and…
Siddhu–Kanhu also spent their childhood in this environment. As time passed and both brothers grew up, they saw that an English officer named Francis Buchanan came to their region and submitted a report highlighting the immense potential of the shifting cultivation (Jhum) system there. Based on this report, his senior Cleveland issued an order. Under this, the forest dwellers were brought under an organized farming system. Along with this, they were also brought under the scope of permanent settlement, which had a negative impact on the local people.
In reality, Jhum cultivation itself was full of difficulties for the forest dwellers. On top of that, the order of permanent settlement pushed them into a severe crisis. This was not all—another exploitative system was created by the British, under which the forest dwellers would not be permitted to extract any resources from the forest, and they would not even be allowed to enter forests for hunting. This system was also implemented in Siddhu–Kanhu’s village Bhognadih. Therefore, it was inevitable that they would be affected. This was the reason that feelings of resentment against the British had already begun to grow in their minds.
The slogan: “Do or Die, British, Leave Our Soil”
Although the first war of independence is considered to have taken place in 1857, even before that, in the Santhal Pargana region of the present-day state of Jharkhand, the British had suffered heavy losses due to the Santhal Hul and the Santhal struggle. The two brothers Siddhu–Kanhu had clearly understood the exploitative policies of the British-supported landlord agents and the British rule.
Under the leadership of the two brothers Siddhu–Kanhu, this struggle began on 30 June 1855 from Bhognadih village of the present Sahibganj district. On this occasion, Siddhu had declared: “Do or Die, British, Leave Our Soil.”
According to historians, the people of Santhal Pargana have, from the beginning, been forest dwellers by nature—religious, lovers of nature, and simple-hearted. The landlords and later the British took full advantage of this. Historians state that in this region the British imposed land revenue (Malguzari) on the Santhal hills and other inhabitants. After this, not only did exploitation of the local people begin, but they were also forced to pay Malguzari. Because of this, a spirit of struggle began to rise among the people here.
Nagpuri litterateur and historian B.P. Keshari states that even if this struggle was the Santhal Hul, it was in fact a freedom movement against the exploitative British, led by all the poor and oppressed people of Santhal Pargana.
Beginning of the Movement: Decision to Stop Paying Malguzari
The leaders of this public movement were four sons of Bhognadih resident Chunni Mandi, who was landless but the village headman. These four sons were Siddhu, Kanhu, Chand, and Bhairav. It is said that these four brothers continuously shaped the people’s dissatisfaction into a movement.
At that time, the Santhals were told that Siddhu had received a message in a dream from Bonga, who had twenty fingers on his hands, and who had declared that the landlords, moneylenders, police, and royal officials should be destroyed. It is said that the Santhals worshipped Bonga itself. This message was spread from village to village by beating the drum (dugdugi). During this period, people travelled from village to village carrying branches of the Sal tree. To give the movement a practical and traditional form, on 30 June 1855, people from four hundred villages gathered in Bhognadih village, and the movement was formally launched. In that very assembly, it was announced that they would no longer pay Malguzari.
The Santhals Gave a Strong Reply to the British
After this, the British ordered the arrest of all four brothers. However, the police officer (daroga) who was sent there was killed by the Santhals by cutting off his neck. During this time, fear spread among government officials regarding the movement. Security in Bhagalpur was tightened. Due to the message of this revolution, British rule in the Santhal region had almost ended.
The British sent an army into the region to suppress the movement. Large-scale arrests were made, and heavy gunfire was directed at the revolutionaries. Martial law was imposed to control the movement. Rewards were announced for capturing the revolutionaries.
In the battle at Barhait, both Chand and Bhairav were martyred in the fight between the British and the revolutionaries. The famous historian Hunter, in his book Annals of Rural (as mentioned in the source), wrote that the Santhals did not know what surrender meant, and therefore the dugdugi continued to sound and the people kept fighting.
As long as even one revolutionary remained alive, he continued to fight. It is written in the book that there was not even a single British soldier who was not ashamed of this sacrifice. In this war, about 20,000 forest dwellers lost their lives.
By bribing some unfaithful companions, Siddhu and Kanhu were also captured, and then on 26 July 1855 both brothers were publicly hanged from a tree in Bhognadih village.
In today’s time, Siddhu–Kanhu’s ninth generation exists, which is still struggling even for two meals a day. Their family was given a tractor by the government, but in the struggle to arrange food, it was also sold, and Siddhu’s family members are working as drivers on that same tractor.
The fierce struggle that these four brothers initiated in Santhal Pargana against landlords and the British makes one’s hair stand on end even by just reading about it in newspapers. At that time, when their family did not even have an inch of land and their father was landless yet the village headman, these great warriors—driven only by unwavering devotion to the motherland and by the suffering of poor people crushed by corrupt and dishonest landlords and cruel British exploitation—fought against the British, about whom it was said that “the sun never set on their empire,” and they shook them on the battlefield.
The tribal society is not only of the entire country but of the whole world. This incomparable sacrifice of these great warriors is a matter of immense pride.
Biography of Siddhu–Kanhu
The Santhal Hul was led by the four sons of Chunni Manjhi, a resident of Bhognadih. They were Siddhu, Kanhu, Chand, and Bhairav. During the Hul, Kanhu’s age was said to be 35 years, Chand’s age 30 years, and Bhairav’s age 20 years. Siddhu was the eldest, but his birth is believed to have been around 1825.
When Siddhu–Kanhu decided to revolt against exploitation, they adopted traditional methods to unite and gather the community. The dugdugi was beaten, and the message of the Sal branch was sent from village to village. The Sal branch is a symbol of the revolutionary message.
The date 30 June 1855 was fixed for a massive assembly rally at Bhognadih. The responsibility of bringing people to the assembly with bows and arrows was given to the Manjhi/Parganas. The message spread everywhere. People began walking from distant villages, and about 20,000 Santhals, armed with bows, arrows, and traditional weapons, reached Bhognadih on 30 June.
In this huge gathering, the slogans of Siddhu–Kanhu—“British, Leave Our Land”—echoed loudly. Tilak had once said, “Freedom is our birthright.” Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose had said, “Give me blood, and I will give you freedom.” Gandhi had given the slogan, “Do or Die.” Even before them, on the same lines, Siddhu had raised the call: “Do or Die, British, Leave Our Soil.”
Siddhu believed in democracy. He understood that a mass movement could not be possible only through the Santhals. Therefore, he also took the support of others. In this rebellion, other people such as Kumhar, Chamar, Gwala, Teli, Lohar, Dom, and Muslims also joined.
To make the Hul active and successful, Siddhu–Kanhu also used religion as support. They promoted the idea of visions of Marang Buru (chief deity) and Jaher–Era (chief goddess), and their order (to establish Abua Raj), to inspire the people’s emotions. The Sal branch invitation was sent door to door, informing people that they must gather in Bhognadih on 30 June to receive the blessings of the chief goddess and deity.
As a result, around 30,000 armed people gathered in Bhognadih on 30 June. In that assembly, Siddhu was appointed as king, Kanhu as minister, Chand as administrator, and Bhairav as commander, and the formation of a new Santhal state was announced.
It was resolved to drive away and destroy the moneylenders, police, landlords, revenue officials, government employees, and the “blue-skinned whites” (British), and also to stop paying taxes and to refuse to obey government orders. The British had encouraged the Santhals for indigo farming, but soon their exploitation began, and it continued to increase. The Santhals were angry with them as well.
Around the same time, an incident occurred. An English contractor associated with railway construction kidnapped three Santhal women labourers. This acted like fuel to the fire. The revolution began. Some Santhals attacked the British. Three British men were killed, and the kidnapped women were freed.
When the Hul march began, it advanced towards Calcutta. Entire villages were looted and burned, and people began to be killed. About 20,000 Santhal youths attacked the Raj Bhavan of Amber Pargana and captured it on 2 July.
The British in Phudnipur, Kadamsar, and Pyalapur were killed. The large bungalows of the British indigo planters were seized. On 7 July, the daroga of Dighi police station and British supporter Mahesh Lal Dutt was killed. By then, 19 people had been put to death.
From the Martel tower built for security, indiscriminate gunfire caused heavy losses to the Santhal soldiers. Even then, bows and arrows continued to fight against guns. The Santhals captured the वीरभूमि (Veerbhumi) region and drove the British away from there. The British soldiers suffered a crushing defeat at Veerpaiti. The biggest Santhal victory occurred in the battles of Raghunathpur and Sangrampur. In this struggle, one European commander, some native officers, and 25 soldiers were killed.
The British Imposed Martial Law
Martial law was implemented in all districts of the Bhagalpur Commissionary. Rewards were announced for the arrest of the rebels. Strong arrangements were also made to confront them.
In the battle of Barhait, Chand and Bhairav weakened and became victims of British bullets. Now some companions of Siddhu–Kanhu, tempted by greed, joined the British. With the help of traitors, Kanhu was finally arrested near Uparbanda village. Siddhu was also captured on 19 August in Barhait.
Mehar Shikvarg (as mentioned in the source) took him prisoner and brought him to Bhagalpur jail. Both brothers were publicly hanged. Siddhu was hanged in Barhait at the place where the daroga had been killed, and Kanhu was hanged in Bhognadih itself. With this, the strong leadership of the Santhal rebellion came to an end.
On 30 November, the Santhal Pargana district was legally established, and Ashley Eden was appointed as its first district judge. Administration of Santhal Pargana began under laws different from the rest of the country.
The Santhal Hul came to an end. However, within just two years, it prepared the foundation for the Sepoy Rebellion of 1857—the First War of Independence. Even today, Chotanagpur takes pride in these four brothers, and Siddhu–Kanhu are still remembered in Santhali songs. The birth anniversary of these martyrs is celebrated as Santhal Hul Day.
Dispute Regarding the Date of Martyrdom
According to most scholars, on 26 July both brothers were publicly hanged from a tree in Bhognadih village. In this way, Siddhu, Kanhu, Chand, and Bhairav—these four brothers—created an unforgettable place for themselves in Indian history forever.

Title: Siddhu–Kanhu: The First Revolt in Santhal Pargana Against the British
In Santhal Pargana, the first major revolt against the British took place. Many years before the birth of Birsa Munda in Jharkhand, in the village of Bhognadih in Santhal Pargana, four brothers—Siddhu, Kanhu, Chand, and Bhairav—and two sisters—Phulo and Jhano—were born in the family of Chunka Murmu. Siddhu was the eldest among the four brothers, and along with Kanhu, he led the Santhal Hul (Santhal Revolt) from 1853 to 1856 against the British Empire.
This was the first struggle in which not only the Santhal tribal people, but also oppressed people from all sections of society came forward under the leadership of Siddhu and Kanhu. The bravery of the four brothers and the two sisters Phulo and Jhano was extraordinary. Riding on horses and carrying Sal leaves, they went from village to village inviting people to join the Hul. Whenever they got an opportunity, they would capture British soldiers and kill them. This can be considered a living example of the anger that existed among the common people against the British. Even today, after so many years, some people may find it difficult to believe, but in tribal society, not only men but women also took the lead in opposing the British.
Siddhu and Kanhu – Leaders of the Masses
This liberation movement led by Siddhu and Kanhu involved seizing the wealth and property of oppressive landlords and moneylenders who were supporters of the British, and Siddhu and Kanhu distributed all that wealth among the poor. On 30 June 1855, a grand public assembly was held in Bhognadih village under the leadership of Siddhu and Kanhu. In this assembly, people from the forest regions of Bhognadih participated, along with nearly ten thousand people from Dumka, Deoghar, Godda, Pakur, Jamtara, Maheshpur, Kahalgaon, Hazaribagh, Manbhum, Bardhaman, Bhagalpur, Purnia, Sagarbhanga, Upbandh, and other nearby communities.
With unanimous consent, Siddhu and Kanhu were chosen as the leaders of all. Their two other brothers, Chand and Bhairav, also sacrificed everything in this struggle. In this assembly, discipline and rules were established. Different responsibilities were assigned to different groups. Hearing this description, the scene of the assembly becomes visible before the eyes, and a feeling of pride arises in the mind due to the information. In tribal society, devotion towards the nation, religion, and culture has been visible since ancient times, and even in the present time, a similar experience can be felt.
A Historic Decision
The intensity of this struggle announced in the public assembly can be understood from the fact that a decision was taken not to cultivate crops that year. Taking such a decision was not an ordinary matter. Siddhu and Kanhu took this decision with the intention that the tribal society could survive by eating roots and tubers, but what would the British (Babu) eat if there was no farming at all?
It is important to note that everyone ate grain, but only the tribal people produced it. Although this decision showed the poor tribals a path of suffering, the passion to fight for life had spread so strongly among them that this decision was also passed unanimously in the public assembly, and cultivation was stopped.
History is witness that in March 1767 AD, in order to stop the advancing armed British forces, the tribal people of Dhalumgarh set fire to their own houses and burned them down, and with bows, arrows, and guerrilla attacks, they did not allow the British to enter their regions from 1767 to 1800 and continued resisting them. The decision taken in the public assembly to stop cultivation was similar to burning down one’s own home.
The decision of the British government to lay a railway line from Kolkata to the iron hills of Santhal Pargana acted like fuel to the fire of the Santhal Revolt of 1855. During the process of laying the railway line, the ancestral lands of the Santhals were taken away without any compensation. Thousands of acres of fertile land were snatched from the Santhals. Poor people were forced into bonded labor for railway construction, and they were forcibly sent to Kolkata.
In history, contractors named Tams and Hens are mentioned, who committed inhuman atrocities on the tribal people—spared neither children, nor the elderly, nor women. Now it had become difficult to tolerate. The question of the community’s self-respect had come forward. Everyone was united that they would not tolerate it any longer, and they decided that this year they would not cut paddy crops, but would cut the necks of the oppressive British and their supporters.
Hul (Revolt)
The land of Santhal Pargana became covered with dead bodies, and rivers of blood began to flow. The sound of cannons and guns shook Santhal Pargana and its surrounding areas. The terror and success of the Santhal Revolt can be understood from the fact that during nearly 200 years of British rule, only this movement was suppressed by imposing Martial Law. Under this, the British committed inhuman atrocities to crush the revolutionaries. Its full description is not found in the pages of history, but glimpses of those inhuman atrocities are still alive today in Jharkhand through Santhali traditions, songs, and stories.
Siddhu and Kanhu Were Hanged
The Santhal Revolt was the first movement in the world in which women also played an equal role. From 30 June 1855 until the end of October, the movement spread throughout the entire region, and revolutionaries gained control over almost all villages. However, in a battle near Maheshpur, Siddhu, Kanhu, and Bhairav were injured, and Siddhu’s wounds were severe. Kanhu and Bhairav recovered through traditional herbal treatment and again jumped into the movement. Siddhu took a little longer to recover, but during this time, a traitor informed the British and Siddhu was arrested. This was the first major blow to the Santhal Revolt.
The second blow came in Jamtara, when the leader of Upbandh, Sardaar Ghatwal, betrayed Kanhu and handed him over to the British army in January 1856. Siddhu had already been hanged by the British. Soon, through a false trial, Kanhu was also hanged. Although Siddhu, Kanhu, and their two other brothers Chand and Bhairav became martyrs in the Santhal Revolt, the British could not completely crush this movement.
In the present time, parents who consider making their sons earn only lakhs and crores of rupees as the purpose of life may find it extremely difficult to imagine that in a single family—Siddhu, Kanhu, Chand, and Bhairav, along with two sisters Phulo and Jhano—everyone sacrificed their lives. The entire family became like the sacrificial firewood of a grand yajna. Tribal society may be considered illiterate in words, but in values and culture it can be called ideal.
The Santhal Revolt can be called the second great revolt of India. The Kol Revolt of 1831 AD was the first great revolt of India, and the Freedom Struggle of 1857 was the third great revolt of India.
The success of the Santhal Revolt of 1855 AD lies in the fact that without demanding it, India’s first tribal division named “Santhal Pargana” was created, and the Santhal Pargana Tenancy Act was formed. There is no doubt that India’s first national movement, the Freedom Struggle of 1857, followed many footprints of the Santhal Revolt.
The importance of the Santhal Hul can be understood from the fact that the contemporary German thinker Karl Marx, in his book “Notes on Indian History,” called the Santhal Hul of June 1855 a “people’s revolution.” However, Indian historians neglected the tribal people and pushed them to the margins of history. As a result, today the contributions of Siddhu, Kanhu, Chand, Bhairav, and their two real sisters Phulo and Jhano cannot be properly read in the pages of history.
Therefore, it is said that if the determination exists in the mind to turn the bright dreams of the future into reality through efforts in the present, then glorious history must definitely be studied. If the tribal society’s struggle for the freedom movement has received little mention in the pages of history, then today’s youth should conduct new research based on documents written by the British and based on folk narratives obtained from villages.

Title: The Past - June 30 Independence Struggle in Santhal Pargana: Hul Movement
The June 30 Santhal Pargana struggle is a milestone in the independence movement. In reality, this war had commenced in 1855. This movement took place in the Santhal Pargana region of present-day Jharkhand. The Santhal Hul (Santhal Hool) or Santhal Rebellion is direct evidence of this. Santhal Pargana is a forest-dweller region with fertile land. Forest dwellers are naturally lovers of religion and nature, and are simple by temperament. Landlords always took advantage of this; it was common practice there to take valuable forest produce and give inexpensive items of equal weight in return. After the arrival of the British, these landlords joined hands with them, and the Santhals began to face a double blow. For domestic needs, tribal individuals would often take loans at 5 percent to 50 percent interest and carry that burden for a lifetime.
In 1784, a brave man of the Santhal tribe named Tilka Manjhi, whom the tribal society calls Baba Tilka Manjhi, fought against the British for a long time with his disciples. The British captured him in Bhagalpur and dragged him by tying him to a horse's tail. When Tilka Manjhi was injured and half-dead, he was hanged from a banyan tree in Bhagalpur. It is said that in this same lineage, Sido-Kanhu (Sidhu-Kanhu), Chand-Bhairav (Chand-Bhairo), and Phulo-Jhano followed. On June 30, 1855, the Santhals initiated this Hul revolution from Panchkathiya (Panchkathia) village, which later spread throughout the entire region. Due to this movement, the British had to retreat in many places and the Santhals were victorious.
There was a time when the message of the revolution reached every house. The result was that British rule almost ended in that area. Encouraged by this, one day fifty thousand Santhal heroes attacked the British and chased them toward Kolkata. Seeing this, the government sent an army under the leadership of Major Burrough. In a 5-hour bloody struggle, the British were defeated and the Santhal heroes took control of the Pakur district. Hundreds of British soldiers were killed. This panicked the company officials. Martial law was imposed across the entire region and it was handed over to the army. Now the British army had a free hand. The British army possessed modern weapons and arms, whereas the Santhal heroes had traditional weapons like bows and arrows; therefore, the tide turned. Rivers of blood began to flow everywhere. In this war, approximately 20,000 forest-dweller heroes sacrificed their lives.
The famous British historian Hunter wrote about this war in his book 'The Annals of Rural Bengal' that the Santhals had no knowledge of a word like surrender. As long as their drum (dhol) kept beating, they continued to fight. As long as even one of them remained, he kept fighting. There was not a single soldier in the British army who was not ashamed of this courage. In this struggle, along with Sidhu and Kanhu, their two other brothers Chand and Bhairav were also killed. In memory of this event, 'Hul Diwas' (Hool Day) is celebrated every year on June 30. Karl Marx, in his book 'Notes on Indian History', has called this event a people's revolution.
