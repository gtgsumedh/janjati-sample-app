Budhu Bhagat
(Born: 17 February 1795 – Died: 13 February 1832)
In Indian history, Veer Budhu Bhagat is known as a famous revolutionary. His struggle was against the atrocities and injustice committed by the British, landlords (Zamindars), and moneylenders. Budhu Bhagat was born on 17 February 1795, into an Oraon family in a village called Silgai in the Ranchi district of present-day Jharkhand. It is said that he possessed divine powers, symbolized by an axe he always carried with him.
As a young boy, Budhu would sit for hours on the banks of the Koel River in Silgai village, thinking of ways to drive out the British and the landlords. Due to his habit of sitting in solitude for hours and his mastery in wielding the sword and bow-and-arrow, people began to view him as a divine messenger. From childhood, Budhu Bhagat witnessed the cruelty of the landlords and the British army. He saw how the landlords would forcibly take away the ready crops. He contemplated how the tribal society could be saved from this British oppression. There were times when the stoves in the homes of poor villagers would not be lit for days. Hearing the brilliant and inspiring words of the young Budhu, the tribal society began to consider him their savior.
Generally, the year 1857 is considered the first struggle for independence. However, before that, Veer Budhu Bhagat had not only sounded the clarion call of revolution but, through his courage and leadership, had also initiated the historic movement known as the 'Larka Rebellion' in 1832. Brutality was at its peak in the tribal areas of Chota Nagpur during the British rule. The Mundas had already launched a fierce war against the landlords and moneylenders. The Oraons also adopted a rebellious stance.
By training the forest dwellers in guerrilla warfare, Budhu Bhagat established his base on the peak of Bogari Hill amidst dense forests. It was here that his war strategies were formulated. People were ready to sacrifice their lives at his single command. Budhu Bhagat now had sufficient public support for the rebellion. It was because of the war waged by Budhu Bhagat against injustice that the C.N.T. (Chota Nagpur Tenancy) Act was created for land protection. He called for an uprising against injustice. Thousands of hands rose with bows and arrows, swords, and axes. Budhu trained the squad captured by Captain Impey for guerrilla warfare. Taking advantage of the dense forests and difficult hilly terrains, he defeated the British army several times. To capture Budhu, the British government announced a reward of 1,000 rupees. However, this greed yielded no results.
The armed struggle of thousands of people made the British government and the landlords tremble. The task of capturing Budhu Bhagat was assigned to Captain Impey. A large contingent of the 50th Native Infantry of Benaras and cavalry soldiers was sent into the forest. Thousands of villagers from Tiku and surrounding villages were arrested. Budhu's squad freed the prisoners in the valley itself. Captain Impey was infuriated by this crushing defeat.
On 13 February 1832, Budhu Bhagat and his companions were surrounded by Captain Impey in Silgai village. Budhu wanted to surrender so that innocent villagers would not be killed in the indiscriminate firing by the British. However, Budhu's followers formed a circular shield around him to protect him. After a warning, the Captain ordered to open fire. Indiscriminate firing began. From all directions, the horrific screams of children, women, and youth were heard. About 300 villagers were killed. The bloody carnage by the British continued. Even hearing the description makes us tremble; imagine the mental strength of those who witnessed or endured it firsthand! We bow to those brave rural people who threw themselves into the jaws of death to protect the life of Budhu Bhagat. The rebellion against injustice was forcibly silenced by the power of weapons. Budhu Bhagat and his sons, Haldhar and Giridhar, also attained martyrdom while fighting the British.
— Inder Bhagat, Ambikapur, Chhattisgarh
