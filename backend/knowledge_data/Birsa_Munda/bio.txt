Bhagwan Birsa Munda 

1. Introduction

Bhagwan Birsa Munda, born on 15 November 1875 in Ulihatu (Jharkhand, Chotanagpur region), was a महान tribal freedom fighter, social reformer, and spiritual leader. Though he lived only 25 years (1875–1900), his contribution to tribal identity, culture, and India’s freedom struggle was extraordinary.

He is remembered as:
  Protector of religion and culture
  Leader of tribal resistance
  Symbol of “Jal, Jungle, Zameen” (Water, Forest, Land rights)

Today, 15 November is celebrated as “Tribal Pride Day (Janajati Gaurav Diwas)” in his honor.

2. Family Background and Early Life
  Father: Sugana Munda
  Mother: Karmi Hatu
  Siblings: Two brothers and two sisters
  Born in a poor tribal farming family

Due to poverty and British exploitation:
  The family migrated from Ulihatu to Bamba
  Faced oppression from British rulers and landlords
  Tribal communities were treated as uncivilized and inferior

Birsa spent his childhood:
  Grazing cattle
  Living a simple tribal life
  Receiving basic education from local teachers like Jaipal Nag

3. Education and Conversion to Christianity
  Studied in German Mission School, Chaibasa
  Family converted to Christianity due to:
  Poverty
  Promises of equality and development
  Birsa was baptized and called “David Birsa”

However, he observed:
  Missionaries insulted tribal culture and Hindu traditions
  Conversion promises were not fulfilled
  Tribal identity was being erased

He strongly opposed:
  Cutting of traditional hair (choti)
  Forced religious practices
  Disrespect toward tribal people

Eventually:
  He protested against a priest who insulted tribals
  He was expelled from the mission school

4. Religious Thinking and Philosophy

Birsa believed:
  No religion teaches hatred
  All religions aim at:
  Humanity
  Truth
  Compassion
  Service

He said:
  People should not abandon their own religion
  All religions are like branches of one tree leading to God

Later:
  He met Anand Pandey (1891)
  Studied:
  Vedas, Upanishads
  Ramayana, Mahabharata, Gita
  Returned to indigenous beliefs and traditions

5. Social and Religious Reform Movement (Birsait)

Birsa started a movement called “Birsait”, which focused on:

Moral and Social Reforms:
  No alcohol consumption
  No चोरी (theft)
  No cow slaughter
  Wear sacred thread
  Plant Tulsi
  Follow discipline and purity

Spiritual Awareness:
  Belief in one God
  Self-purification
  Ethical lifestyle

Result:
  Thousands of tribals joined him
  He became a spiritual leader
  People started worshipping him as “Bhagwan”

6. Awareness Against British and Missionaries

Birsa realized:
  British + Missionaries + Landlords = Combined exploitation system

He declared:
“Sahib Sahib Ek Topi”
(British rulers and missionaries are the same)

He spread awareness that:
  Missionaries were tools of British control
  Tribals were being exploited economically and culturally

7. Social Reform Demands

Birsa demanded:
  End of forced labor (Begari)
  Stop economic exploitation
  Fair wages for labor
  Protection laws for tribals
  Punishment for oppressors
  Equal facilities like other communities
  Tribal rights over:
  Forests
  Land
  Natural resources
  Tax-free land
  Safety of tribal women
  Education for children
  Freedom to visit temples
  Ban on forced religious conversions

These demands later influenced Indian constitutional protections for tribal communities.

8. Organization and Leadership
  Formed tribal youth groups
  United Munda, Oraon, and other tribes
  Spread awareness village-to-village
  Established:
  Ashrams
  Health centers
  Revolutionary centers

He gave the slogan:
“Abua Dishom, Abua Raj”
(Our land, our rule)

9. Ulgulan – The Great Tribal Revolt

Birsa launched a revolution called:

“Ulgulan” (The Great Uprising)

Objectives:
  End British rule
  Protect tribal land
  Restore tribal dignity

Key Actions:
  Refusal to pay taxes
  Attacks on police stations
  Armed resistance using:
  Bows
  Arrows
  Stones

10. Major Conflicts

1. Khunti Attack (1897)
  Birsa and 400 followers attacked Khunti police station

2. Tanga River Battle (1898)
  Tribals initially defeated British forces

3. Dombari Hill Massacre (9 January 1900)
  Thousands gathered for a meeting
  British surrounded the hill
  Open firing killed:
  Men
  Women
  Children

This massacre is compared to Jallianwala Bagh.

11. British Reaction and Reports

British reports accused Birsa of:
  Preventing tribals from farming
  Encouraging rejection of Christianity
  Promoting rebellion
  Claiming control over forests

British government:
  Issued arrest warrant under legal sections
  Declared reward for his capture

12. Arrest
  Arrested on 3 February 1900 at Chakradharpur / Bandgaon region
  Captured when many followers were away
  Despite resistance, he was overpowered

After arrest:
  Thousands of tribals gathered in protest
  British feared mass rebellion

13. Imprisonment in Ranchi Jail
  Presented before Deputy Commissioner
  Transferred to Ranchi Jail

Even in jail:
  People continued worshipping him
  His influence kept growing

British officials stated:
  His release could cause a rebellion like 1857 revolt

14. Death
  Date: 9 June 1900
  Place: Ranchi Jail

Official reason:
  Cholera

Believed by people:
  Poisoning by British authorities

His body:
  Secretly cremated near a drain
  No public ceremony allowed

15. Contribution to Freedom Struggle

Birsa’s contributions include:
  Organized tribal resistance
  Challenged British authority
  Revived cultural identity
  Fought social evils
  Inspired future revolutionaries

He is considered:
  Tribal hero
  Freedom fighter
  Spiritual reformer

16. Titles and Recognition

He is known as:
  Bhagwan Birsa Munda
  Dharti Aba (Father of Earth)

People worship him for:
  Courage
  Leadership
  Spiritual power

17. Legacy

His movement led to:
  Awakening of tribal consciousness
  Resistance against exploitation
  Foundation for tribal rights laws

Even today:
  His teachings inspire unity and self-respect
  Tribal communities honor him deeply

18. Tribal Pride Day

Government of India declared:

15 November = Janajati Gaurav Diwas

Purpose:
  Honor Birsa Munda
  Remember tribal freedom fighters
  Recognize tribal contributions

19. Conclusion

Bhagwan Birsa Munda’s life represents:
  Courage against oppression
  Faith in culture and identity
  Fight for justice and equality

Though his life was short, his impact was long-lasting.
He remains a guiding force for tribal pride and national history.
