Ramji Gond – A Brave Tribal Freedom Fighter During the struggle for independence, resistance against the British was taking place across the country. The tribal regions were also not behind in this movement. In present-day Telangana and the then Andhra Pradesh, the Gond community also raised their voice, declaring that they would not accept the rule of the British or the Nizam who supported them. Many Gond youths came forward, and one of them was Ramji Gond. Ramji Gond was born in Sonkhas village of Adilabad district. From childhood, he observed the events happening around him and was deeply disturbed by the atrocities faced by villagers. In his youth, he was influenced by the ideas of Nana Sahib Peshwa. He led rural youth and participated in the freedom struggle against the British. Although he was only 24 years old, through his courage and the collective strength of the Gond, Rohilla, and other rural communities, he posed a strong challenge to the British. In the beginning, Ramji Gond successfully used guerrilla warfare techniques for more than two years in the dense forest regions stretching from Nirmal–Narayan Khed in the west to the Godavari River in the south and Chennoor in the east. Due to his bravery and leadership, there was a time when he was regarded almost like a ruler of Nirmal city and its surrounding areas. The people under his leadership lived happily. At that time, the Nizam was allied with the British, so Ramji Gond also fought against the Nizam. Near Nirmal city, the British hanged thousands of Gond and Rohilla warriors on a banyan tree to spread fear among the people. For the sake of maintaining their power, the British could do anything. There was no limit to their cruelty and oppression. Local people know this place as the “Tree of Thousand Hangings.” Ramji Gond was also executed on the same tree. In the future, this tree became a source of inspiration for many, including Kumaram Bheem. The then British Resident of Hyderabad requested the Nirmal Talukdar (Collector) to arrest Ramji Gond. Under the leadership of Colonel Robert from Hingoli, the 47th Regiment and another regiment from Bellary, Karnataka, arrived to assist. This shows how powerful Ramji Gond’s army must have been. The conflict was long-lasting. The British received information that Ramji Gond’s army had taken shelter near Nirmal city. The region was very difficult terrain, but it was familiar to Ramji Gond’s forces, which gave them some advantage. Although Ramji Gond’s army was smaller compared to the British, they fought with full strength. Additional support from the Nizam also aided the British. In the fierce battle, traditional weapons like bows and arrows could not match modern weapons. Many villagers were killed, and eventually Ramji Gond was captured. This was what the British wanted. He was hanged on the same “Tree of Thousand Hangings.” Thus, a tribal warrior attained martyrdom in the freedom struggle. Years later, the struggle led by Kumaram Bheem was inspired by Ramji Gond. (Reference available in the State Archives Office, Hyderabad, Telangana.)
