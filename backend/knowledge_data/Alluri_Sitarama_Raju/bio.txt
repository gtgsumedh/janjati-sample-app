Alluri Sitarama Raju
(Born: 4 July 1897 – Died: 7 May 1924)
Alluri Sitarama Raju was born on 4 July 1897 in Mogallu village, West Godavari district of Andhra Pradesh, India. He received his early education in Rajahmundry and Rajachandrapuram. His mind was more inclined toward spirituality than formal studies. He studied Ayurveda and astrology, but his interest did not remain there either. Therefore, for social service, he adopted the life of a monk and set out on a journey across India.
During his travels throughout India, he experienced the pain of slavery under British rule. He was a devotee of Goddess Kali. During a pilgrimage, he came into contact with revolutionaries in Chittagong, and from there his life changed completely. He learned horse riding, archery, yoga, and other skills.
He then made freedom for the country, along with service to the poor, the goal of his life. He started his spiritual practice at the Neelkantheshwar Temple in Krishnadevi Peta. Rising above caste differences, in a short time he successfully built connections and unity among the Koya and Chenchu tribal communities living nearby.
He planted the seed of resistance against slavery in the hearts of the tribal people. The people were already suffering from British oppression, so they joined the struggle. Tribal youth began fighting the British army using their traditional weapons such as bows, arrows, spears, and axes.
In August 1922, they attacked the Chintapalli police station and captured it. They obtained a large number of modern weapons, and British police officers were also injured.
Raju’s courage continued to grow. He began attacking police stations openly in daylight. One of his revolutionary companions was Beerayya Dora. When the police were taking him from jail to court, Raju attacked with his group and freed him. After this, the British government announced a reward of 10,000 rupees for anyone who captured Raju. Even then, he continued to challenge British rule for many years.
In several areas of Andhra, British control ended and Raju’s influence spread. He established village panchayats, due to which local disputes stopped going to British courts. People also stopped paying taxes to the British administration.
British officers realized that if Raju’s activities were not controlled, they would completely lose control. To fight him, they brought troops from Assam to the Godavari district. Raju and his followers continued fighting with traditional weapons. In the battle of Peddavalasa, his side suffered heavy losses, but Raju managed to escape.
The British then started torturing innocent villagers. Seeing this, Raju could not tolerate it and surrendered himself. This was exactly what the British wanted.
On 7 May 1924, the British tied him to a tree and shot him dead.
Thus ended the life of a revolutionary, monk, devotee of God, and social reformer.
