Raghoji Bhangre
Birth:November 8, 1805 | Death: May 2, 1848

The contribution of our tribal brethren in the efforts to achieve independence is incomparable. The Mahadev Koli community resides in districts like Nashik, Trimbakeshwar, Nagar (Ahmednagar), and Poona in Maharashtra. The people of the Mahadev Koli tribe are known for being extremely hardworking and brave. A brilliant boy was born into a family of this very Mahadev Koli tribe. Raghoji was born on November 8, 1805, in a small village called Devgaon (District Nagar, Maharashtra). His mother's name was Ramabai and his father's name was Ramji. His father was employed by the British as a Subedar of the Rajur province. Ramji Bhangre, along with Govindrao Khade, the fort commander (Kiledar) of Ratangad, started a rebellion. Raghoji was young himself, but he understood everything. He was aware of his father's rebellion. Suppressing the movement started by Ramji, the British sent many to jail. In this sequence, Ramji was sentenced to life imprisonment and sent to 'Kalapani' (Cellular Jail in Andaman). History says that there were thousands of such brave heroes who were sentenced to life imprisonment and spent their days until the very end in the Andaman jail. We neither know the names of many freedom fighters nor their courageous efforts. Born into the family of such a valiant Ramji Bhangre, Raghoji is one of the freedom fighters of Maharashtra.

Raghoji's father was sent to Andaman to serve the Kalapani sentence. Fearing that Raghoji Bhangre might stand up in rebellion because of this incident, the British employed him in a high-ranking position. While in service, various atrocities were committed against him to force him to give statements favorable to the British. Upon learning about this, Raghoji became furious and went straight to the Rajur police station. A heated argument took place between him and the station officer (Amaldar), Amritrao. In retaliation, Raghoji grabbed Amritrao by the neck and killed him instantly. Because Raghoji was enraged and possessed a wrestler-like physique, no one dared to catch him. Raghoji looted all the guns from the police station, mounted a horse, and rode away. Taking his mother's blessings, he left home and took to the jungles. At that time, he was accompanied by Rama Thakur and Devji Awhad. With the resolve to fight against the British like his father, a tribal revolutionary hero was born, who initiated a mass movement.

Raghoji himself was skilled in wielding various weapons and had a knack for mobilizing the youth; hence, hundreds of young men from different tribes accepted him as their leader and joined his army. Within a few days, thousands of youths from regions like the coastal areas of Mula and Pravara rivers, Ratangad, Harishchandragad, Alang, Kulang, Kalsubai, Akole, Rajur, and Sangamner became his comrades in the struggle. The forest region found leadership in Raghoji Bhangre and Bapu Bhangre. The war cry of 'Har Har Mahadev' echoed once again in the Sahyadri mountain ranges. Raghoji's bands began raising their voices against the unjust and oppressive British government, moneylenders, and landlords. They would loot the wealth and grain of the moneylenders and distribute it among the poor, and also burn the forged papers and land documents of the poor that were in the landlords' possession. The public perception began to form that "Raghoji alone is our savior." Raghoji Bhangre conducted raids at several places and helped the poor and the farmers. His companions had immense respect for women. He never forgave any undignified conduct by anyone in his group. Valor and bravery, compassion for farmers and the poor, respect for women, and strict action against misconduct—the entire description seems reminiscent of the era of Shivaji Maharaj.

In 1830, Raghoji planned an armed revolution against the British at several places simultaneously. In the first historic battle near Junnar against a British army of 20,000, Raghoji fought with merely 1,000 companions and killed a total of fifteen thousand white soldiers. The white men began to fear Raghoji's name; the oppressive British regime grew terrified. The years 1844-45 were the most successful and glorious years of the armed revolution by Raghoji and his army. During this time, Raghoji also played a role in the efforts being made to reinstate the deposed Bhosale Chhatrapati of Satara. His name began to be counted among the leading figures.

In 1828, the British increased tax collection on agriculture in the rural areas of Maharashtra. Consequently, discontent grew in society. In 1830, the British hanged Rama Kirwa of Akola tehsil, who was a companion of Raghoji, in the Ahmednagar jail.

The Gazetteer of Thane, Maharashtra, records that in October 1843, Raghoji's comrades descended from the hilly regions and conducted raids in various places. During the period from November 1844 to March 1845, Raghoji's movement was at its peak. In the year 1838 A.D., the flames of rebellion flared up in the premises of Ratangad and Sanagar forts. Captain Mackintosh made great efforts to suppress this rebellion. Information was gathered about where all the paths in the jungles and mountains led. All the secret details related to the rebellion were discovered. Even after doing all this, it was not possible to control the rebellion. The revolt became even more intense than before. The British called in more soldiers to their army. 80 people were imprisoned. There was an atmosphere of fear everywhere. Bapu Bhangre was a close friend. Due to an informant, Bapu Bhangre was killed by the British. Raghoji was enraged. The British placed a bounty of ₹5,000 for his capture.

One incident is highly inspiring. A comrade of Raghoji, Bhavani Pedhekar, fell into the hands of the British. Taking him along, the British were searching for Raghoji. The task was not so simple, but with Bhavani in their grasp, the British were hopeful that Raghoji would definitely be caught. Bhavani led the British to a high cliff and jumped from there, sacrificing his life, but he did not give away Raghoji's whereabouts. Such companions were Raghoji's true strength.

Along with virtues like courage, valor, and morality, Raghoji was a deeply religious man. He had great devotion to Lord Mahadev. He would always go to seek the blessings of the Jyotirlingas at Bhimashankar and Trimbakeshwar, and the Goddess at Vajreshwari.

The Battle of Junnar is famous in history. The British entered the battlefield under the leadership of Captain Mackintosh, but they suffered heavy losses. After taking some rest, they launched a more powerful attack than before. Now Raghoji had to retreat for some time. According to his strategy, intending to retreat slightly and strike again, Raghoji wandered disguised as an ascetic (Sadhu) for a long time. The unfortunate moment arrived when the British captured him during his pilgrimage to Pandharpur and kept him imprisoned in the Thane jail. Raghoji was self-respecting from the very beginning. When he found no lawyer to plead his case in court, he cross-examined his own case himself. History states that on May 2, 1848, Raghoji was hanged in the Thane jail of Maharashtra. A tribal hero who fought in the freedom struggle faded into the flow of time.

The son of a revolutionary father, an organizer who mobilized the masses in the forests and mountains of Maharashtra, and a radiant hero who made many stand up and fight against the atrocities of the British, departed after presenting his ideal role. If such self-respecting and valiant characters are presented before the youth of today, there is no doubt that they will inspire everyone even after so many years.
