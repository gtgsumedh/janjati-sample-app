Birth: Garudapalli, Visakhapatnam (Andhra Pradesh)
Death: May 5, 1959

Marri Kamayya belonged to the Paderu region of Andhra Pradesh.
India is a land of diverse languages and dialects, yet the feeling that “we are all one” was strongly experienced during the freedom movement and continues even today. There are many such names in history that we may not even know, yet we can certainly draw inspiration from them. Garudapalli village in the Hukumpeta mandal of the Paderu region in Andhra Pradesh is known in history because of one such brave individual—Marri Kamayya. He belonged to the Konda Dora tribe.

In this tribal region, there were moneylenders who exploited the simple forest dwellers by collecting excessive taxes, while basic facilities such as roads, water, and education were not provided. It was a form of open exploitation. Plans were made to stand against this exploitation—against the moneylenders and, through them, against the British. A hope arose that a new dawn would come.

Inspired by the Rampa Rebellion of 1924 led by Sitarama Raju, Kamayya began his own efforts. In villages where the Konda tribe lived, he formed village committees and started schools. Today, various efforts are being made for the education of tribal communities. Even in remote forest areas, centers for informal education are being opened along with formal education. Many years ago, similar efforts were made by members of the tribal community themselves. Along with this, efforts toward self-reliance were also undertaken. Attempts were made to increase people’s income, and the community began to organize. To overcome illiteracy and addiction, they adopted Buddhist teachings and followed a simple and pure path called “Alekh.”

The British began taking action against Marri Kamayya. First, they burned down Garudapalli village. Taking along 360 affected families, he established a new village named Beetagurudu. The British continued their oppression there as well. All of Kamayya’s property was confiscated and auctioned. For a long time, he wandered in the forests with his companions. Imagine the suffering of a tribal youth whose home had been burned and who had to live in forests—what must have been the pain in his heart! Even then, he formed a group of 50 people and began resisting the British. On the charge of leading this resistance, Marri Kamayya was arrested and sent to prison. The 11 days of harsh imprisonment were filled with severe torture. After his release, he was again arrested on charges of participating in the Non-Cooperation Movement.

After being released this time, he went underground. He kept changing his base repeatedly and continued his struggle. A tragic incident occurred when his daughter was swept away while crossing a river right before his eyes. This was a great shock, yet he did not step back from the movement. Enduring all personal hardships, he continued his struggle against the British. This struggle continued until India gained independence, and in 1959, this warrior breathed his last.

Salutations to Marri Kamayya, a brave tribal hero.
