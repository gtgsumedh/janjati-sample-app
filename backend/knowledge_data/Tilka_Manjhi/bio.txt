## Detailed Summary of Tilka Manjhi

Tilka Manjhi was one of the earliest tribal revolutionaries and freedom fighters in Indian history. He is remembered as the first tribal leader to raise an organized armed rebellion against British colonial rule in India, nearly ninety years before the Revolt of 1857. His courage, leadership, and sacrifice made him a legendary figure in the history of India’s freedom struggle.

Tilka Manjhi was born on 11 February 1750 in Tilakpur village near Sultanganj in present-day Bihar, India. He belonged to the Santhal tribal community, and his father’s name was Sundara Murmu. He was also known as Jabra Pahadia because of his fearless nature and bravery.

The word “Tilka” in the local language refers to a person with angry or fiery eyes, while “Manjhi” means village headman in the Santhal community. These names reflected both his leadership role and his determined personality.

From childhood, Tilka Manjhi lived close to forests, hills, and rivers. He became skilled in archery, hunting, wrestling, climbing trees, and surviving in difficult environments. The harsh life of the forests made him physically strong, courageous, and capable of fighting in challenging conditions. His deep connection with nature and tribal traditions shaped his character and leadership qualities.

During his youth, Tilka Manjhi witnessed the severe oppression of tribal communities by British officials, landlords, and moneylenders. The British East India Company forcefully occupied tribal lands, forests, agricultural fields, and natural resources. Tribal people were heavily taxed, exploited, and often tortured. Women, children, and elderly villagers also suffered under colonial rule.

These injustices deeply affected Tilka Manjhi and inspired him to organize resistance against the British. He believed that the land and forests belonged to the tribal communities and that foreign rulers had no right to exploit them.

Tilka Manjhi began his rebellion from a place called Banaichari Jor. Under his leadership, tribal warriors organized themselves across Bhagalpur, Sultanganj, Rajmahal hills, and Santhal Pargana. His movement soon became a major challenge for the British administration.

He adopted guerrilla warfare techniques to fight against the British army. Since the tribal fighters had very limited weapons, they used traditional arms such as bows, arrows, spears, and axes. Tilka Manjhi and his followers attacked British soldiers from forests, mountains, valleys, and hidden locations, making it difficult for the British army to control the region.

During the famine of 1770, Tilka Manjhi reportedly attacked British treasuries and distributed food and wealth among poor and starving tribal people. This made him a heroic figure among local communities.

One of his famous declarations against the British was:

> “This land is our mother. We will not pay taxes on our own land.”

As the rebellion grew stronger, the British appointed Augustus Cleveland as the superintendent and magistrate of Rajmahal to suppress the uprising. Cleveland tried to strengthen British control over the tribal regions through military force and administrative pressure.

The most famous event of Tilka Manjhi’s struggle occurred on 13 January 1784. According to historical accounts, Tilka Manjhi climbed a tree near Bhagalpur and attacked Augustus Cleveland with arrows while he was riding on horseback. Cleveland was seriously wounded and later died. This incident shocked the British administration and created fear among British officials throughout the region.

After Cleveland’s death, the British launched a massive operation to capture Tilka Manjhi. One night, while Tilka Manjhi and his companions were celebrating a traditional festival with music and dance, a traitor named Jaudah helped the British launch a surprise attack. Many tribal warriors were killed or arrested, although Tilka Manjhi managed to escape temporarily into the forests and hills near Sultanganj.

The British surrounded the hilly regions and blocked supplies of food and resources. Due to hunger, exhaustion, and lack of weapons, Tilka Manjhi’s army became weaker over time. Eventually, after intense fighting, he was captured by British forces.

The British treated him with extreme cruelty. He was tied with ropes to four horses and dragged through the streets of Bhagalpur as a warning to others. Even after severe torture, Tilka Manjhi remained fearless and defiant.

In 1785, the British hanged Tilka Manjhi from a banyan tree in Bhagalpur. His martyrdom became one of the earliest symbols of resistance against British colonialism in India.

Tilka Manjhi’s sacrifice inspired future generations of freedom fighters and tribal movements across India. He is remembered not only as a brave warrior but also as a symbol of tribal pride, resistance, justice, and freedom.

To honor his memory:

* A statue of Tilka Manjhi has been installed near the court area in Bhagalpur.
* Tilka Manjhi Bhagalpur University was named after him.

Today, Tilka Manjhi is remembered as one of the earliest heroes of India’s independence movement and a powerful voice of tribal resistance against injustice and colonial exploitation.
